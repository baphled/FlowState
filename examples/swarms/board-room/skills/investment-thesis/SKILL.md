# Investment Thesis Skill

## Purpose

This skill defines the structure and evidence standards for a rigorous VC-style investment thesis. Apply it when writing a thesis (bull-analyst, financial-analyst) or when evaluating whether another analyst's thesis meets these standards.

---

## 1. Thesis Statement Format

A valid investment thesis MUST follow this template:

> "We invest because **[unique insight]** creates **[durable advantage]** for **[specific market]**, which **[founders with X]** are best positioned to capture."

Each bracketed element must be specific and non-generic:

| Element | Bad (generic) | Good (specific) |
|---|---|---|
| unique insight | "the market is growing" | "B2B procurement approvals take 47 days on average; no incumbent has attacked the approval workflow layer" |
| durable advantage | "first mover advantage" | "proprietary spend data creates pricing benchmarks competitors cannot replicate without 3+ years of transaction history" |
| specific market | "enterprise software" | "UK mid-market procurement teams at companies with 200–2,000 employees" |
| founders with X | "experienced team" | "ex-Coupa and ex-HSBC procurement leads who have built and sold this workflow before" |

---

## 2. Evidence Hierarchy

When making claims in an investment thesis, classify every supporting statement by its evidence tier:

| Tier | Label | Definition | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Primary data** | Revenue, user counts, retention rates, NPS, CAC, LTV — stated directly by the company | Highest |
| 2 | **Secondary data** | Published market reports, regulatory filings, comparable company exits, analyst research | High |
| 3 | **Analogical reasoning** | "Company X in adjacent market Y achieved Z; this company is in a similar position" | Medium |
| 4 | **Inference** | Logical deductions from stated facts not directly confirmed | Low |
| 5 | **Speculation** | Assumptions with no supporting data | Must be labelled |

**Rule:** Any claim labelled Tier 5 (speculation) that is load-bearing for the investment thesis is a RED FLAG. Flag it explicitly.

---

## 3. Conviction Scoring (1–5)

Assign a conviction score to the investment position as a whole:

| Score | Label | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Weak / Speculative** | Thesis rests primarily on Tier 4–5 evidence; multiple material unknowns |
| 2 | **Possible** | Thesis is coherent but lacks primary data validation; 2+ material risks unaddressed |
| 3 | **Probable** | Thesis supported by secondary data and at least one primary data point; risks identified and manageable |
| 4 | **High Conviction** | Thesis supported by multiple primary data points; differentiation is clear; risks are well-understood and de-risked |
| 5 | **Exceptional** | Breakout evidence (outsized traction, proprietary moat, exceptional team); rare — use sparingly |

**Rule:** Do not give a conviction score of 4 or 5 without at least two Tier 1 (primary data) evidence items.

---

## 4. Insight vs Observation

The most valuable part of an investment thesis is the **insight** — the non-obvious observation that the market has not fully priced in.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Observation** | Something everyone can see from public data | "The procurement software market is worth $12B" |
| **Insight** | A non-obvious truth about timing, mechanics, or positioning that creates asymmetric opportunity | "ERP vendors have deliberately left the approval workflow layer under-specified because it sits between departments — no single VP owns it, so no RFP ever gets written for it" |

**Test for insight:** Would a smart, well-read investor already know this from reading TechCrunch? If yes, it is an observation. If no, it might be an insight.

**Rule:** A thesis with no genuine insight is a weak thesis regardless of market size or team quality.
